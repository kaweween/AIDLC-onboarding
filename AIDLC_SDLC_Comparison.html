⚙️ Process & Rituals
Traditional SDLC / Agile-Scrum
AIDLC (AI-Driven)
Work cycle
Sprint — fixed 2-week iteration. Team commits to a sprint backlog at the start. No changes mid-sprint.
⟳ Fixed cadence
Bolt — variable duration (hours to days). Scoped to a single feature or unit of work. No fixed time-box.
✓ Faster delivery ⚠ Harder to plan capacity AIDLC wins

Why Bolts are shorter: AI can generate a complete feature's requirements, design, code, and tests in hours — not weeks. The 2-week sprint was calibrated to human development speed. There's no reason to maintain that timebox when AI accelerates every stage.

Risk: Without a fixed sprint boundary, capacity planning and resource allocation become harder. Teams need new ways to forecast effort (AI-based complexity estimation) and communicate progress to stakeholders (continuous demo rather than sprint review).

For our team: Start with "soft Bolts" — retain 2-week reporting cycles to management while using AIDLC internally. Gradually reduce the reporting cadence as confidence builds.

Planning & Elaboration
Sprint Planning + Backlog Grooming — PO writes stories, team estimates, SM facilitates. Multiple sessions. 2–4 hours per sprint.
⟳ Human-authored
Mob Elaboration — AI drafts requirements and stories from a PO intent statement. Whole team validates in one 90–120 min session.
✓ Faster, higher completeness ⚠ PO must prepare clear intent AIDLC wins

Why AIDLC wins: AI generates stories at a speed and completeness that would take a PO days to match. It asks clarifying questions that surface hidden requirements the team would otherwise only discover during development. Edge cases, NFRs, and acceptance criteria are generated automatically.

Risk: If the PO's intent statement is vague, the AI generates vague requirements. "Garbage in, garbage out" still applies. Teams need to invest in writing strong problem statements — this is a new skill for many POs.

For Azure DevOps: AI-generated stories can be pushed directly into Azure DevOps work items via GitHub Copilot. The manual story creation step in ADO boards is eliminated.

Daily rituals
Daily stand-up — 15 min. What did I do, what will I do, any blockers? Status-oriented.
⟳ Status reporting
Mob check-in (optional) — Shorter, ad-hoc. Focus on human blockers to AI approval requests. AI maintains context continuity between sessions automatically.
✓ Less ceremony overhead ⚠ Team visibility may decrease

Why stand-ups change: AIDLC's persistent artefacts (aidlc-state.md, audit.md) provide real-time visibility into what AI has done and what's waiting for human input. Status reporting becomes less necessary because status is always visible.

Risk: Some teams use stand-ups for social bonding and team cohesion, not just status. Eliminating them entirely without replacement can hurt team culture. Recommend keeping a 10-min daily touchpoint even if status reporting is reduced.

Review & Retrospective
Sprint Review (stakeholder demo) + Retrospective — every 2 weeks. Fixed calendar events.
⟳ Fixed cadence
Continuous demo as Bolts complete + Retrospective kept. Retrospective now also reviews AI quality, steering rule improvements, approval latency.
✓ Faster stakeholder feedback ⚠ Stakeholder availability needed

Why continuous demos work: When a feature completes in 2 days, waiting 12 days for a sprint review to show it is wasteful. Stakeholders get faster feedback loops, which means requirements errors are caught earlier.

Risk: Stakeholders may feel overwhelmed by the pace. POs need to manage stakeholder availability and set expectations about the new cadence before the transition.

AIDLC retrospectives: Add new agenda items — AI quality (did AI generate correct code?), steering rule gaps (what did AI consistently get wrong?), approval latency (who was blocking approvals?).

👥 Roles & Responsibilities
Traditional SDLC / Agile-Scrum
AIDLC (AI-Driven)
Developer / Engineer
Primary producer — writes code, documentation, unit tests. Time split: 60–70% coding, 20–30% meetings/admin, 10% review.
Quality gatekeeper — reviews and approves AI-generated code, guides AI through Construction phase, updates steering rules.
✓ More strategic work ⚠ Must understand AI output

Key shift: Developers go from spending most of their time writing code to spending most of their time reviewing, guiding, and improving AI-generated code. This is a fundamentally different skill set — closer to technical leadership than solo coding.

Benefit: Developers spend less time on routine implementation (CRUD endpoints, boilerplate, configuration) and more time on complex problem-solving, system design, and architectural decisions.

Risk: Junior developers may not develop fundamental coding skills if they only review AI code. Teams should maintain deliberate practice of coding from scratch alongside AIDLC.

Rule: Every developer must understand and be able to explain every line of AI-generated code that they approve to merge. No exceptions.

Product Owner
Story author — writes user stories, acceptance criteria, refines backlog. Significant documentation time.
Intent provider + validator — writes problem statements, validates AI-generated stories, makes prioritisation decisions.
✓ Less documentation, more decision-making ⚠ Must write clear intent statements AIDLC wins

Key shift: PO's time shifts from writing (stories, acceptance criteria) to validating (AI-generated stories, acceptance criteria). The cognitive load changes — less writing, more judgment.

New critical skill: Writing effective intent statements. A vague intent statement produces vague requirements. POs who master this see the biggest time savings.

Bottleneck risk: POs are the primary approval gate for the Inception phase. If POs are unavailable or slow to approve, the entire team's velocity is zero. AIDLC requires POs to be more available and responsive, not less.

Architect
System designer — creates architecture documents, reviews PRs, participates in design meetings. Often bottleneck.
AI guide + context owner — maintains semantic context documents, approves AI-proposed architectures in Mob Construction, encodes standards in steering rules.
✓ Less solo design work ⚠ Must keep steering rules current

Key shift: Architects move from designing in isolation to guiding AI proposals in real-time. The "blank page" problem largely disappears — AI always provides a starting proposal.

New responsibility: Architects become owners of the "semantic context" — a curated document of architectural decisions, patterns, constraints, and standards that AI uses as its context. This needs regular maintenance.

Benefit: AI-generated architecture proposals are always based on the full requirements context (from Inception), not just what the architect was told in a brief meeting. Architectural decisions are better informed.

Scrum Master
Ceremony facilitator — runs sprint planning, stand-ups, reviews, retrospectives. Removes impediments.
Mob session facilitator — facilitates Mob Elaboration and Mob Construction sessions, monitors approval latency, manages human-AI collaboration rhythm.
⟳ Role evolves, not eliminated ⚠ New facilitation skills needed

Key shift: Many traditional ceremonies disappear or shrink. SMs spend less time running fixed-calendar events and more time facilitating dynamic, AI-involved collaborative sessions.

New metric to own: Approval latency — the time between AI requesting human input and receiving it. High latency = low velocity. SM should track and report this weekly.

New facilitation skill: Managing human-AI sessions requires different skills than managing human-only sessions. SMs need to learn how to time-box AI interactions, structure multi-role input to AI prompts, and keep Mob sessions productive.

📄 Artefacts & Documentation
Traditional SDLC / Agile-Scrum
AIDLC (AI-Driven)
Requirements
Human-written user stories in Azure DevOps. Variable quality. Documentation is often incomplete or out of date.
AI-generated requirements.md committed to repo. Comprehensive, structured, automatically linked to code. Human-approved.
✓ More complete, always up to date AIDLC wins

AIDLC auto-generates detailed requirements including functional requirements, edge cases, non-functional requirements, and acceptance criteria. These are stored in version control alongside the code, making traceability automatic. In traditional SDLC, requirements and code often diverge over time because requirements live in a separate system (Azure DevOps) and are not updated as code evolves.

Design documents
Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) manually written by architects. Often delayed or skipped for small features.
AI-generated design artefacts (design.md) for every feature, based on approved requirements. Architect approves.
✓ Design documentation for every feature ⚠ Potential for design document inflation AIDLC wins

In traditional SDLC, design documentation is often skipped for "smaller" features. This is one of the primary sources of technical debt — decisions are made without documentation, and future maintainers have no record of why something was built a certain way. AIDLC generates design artefacts for every feature, making this the default rather than the exception.

Risk: If AI generates verbose design documents for trivial changes, teams may start ignoring them. Teams should configure steering rules to calibrate design document depth to work item complexity.

Audit trail
Azure DevOps work item history + Git commit history. Gap between requirement decision and code implementation is often unclear.
audit.md — timestamped log of every AI action, every question asked, every human approval. Automatically generated and committed.
✓ Full decision trail, automatic AIDLC wins

The audit.md file is one of AIDLC's most valuable outputs for regulated environments. It provides a complete record of who approved what, when, and after what context. This is typically very difficult to reconstruct in traditional SDLC where decisions are made in verbal conversations or ephemeral chat messages.

Compliance note: For teams with regulatory requirements (SOC 2, ISO 27001, GDPR), the AIDLC audit log may significantly strengthen your compliance posture. Consult your compliance team about using it as part of your audit evidence.

🔧 Tooling — Our Stack (Azure DevOps · Jenkins · VS · MS365)
Traditional SDLC / Agile-Scrum
AIDLC (AI-Driven)
IDE usage (VS / XCode)
Developer writes code manually. AI used as passive autocomplete (Copilot suggestions, occasional chat).
GitHub Copilot in VS becomes the primary AIDLC interface. AIDLC triggered via chat. Rules loaded from .github/copilot-instructions.md.
✓ No new IDE required Compatible with our stack

A key advantage for our team: we don't need to change our IDE. Visual Studio with GitHub Copilot Business/Enterprise already supports the AIDLC workflow. The steering rules are loaded from the repo, so every developer who opens the repo gets AIDLC automatically.

XCode users: the AIDLC workflow also works with Claude via CLAUDE.md at project root, or with any agent that reads project-level instruction files. XCode users may use CLI-based agents alongside XCode.

Azure DevOps (backlog)
PO manually creates work items, stories, acceptance criteria. Team updates status. Sprint boards managed manually.
AI-generated work items pushed to Azure DevOps via Copilot integration. Status updated automatically as AIDLC phases progress. aidlc-docs/ folder in repo links back to ADO items.
✓ Less manual ADO management ⚠ Integration setup required

The Azure DevOps integration with GitHub Copilot allows AI-generated stories and tasks to be pushed directly into your ADO boards. This eliminates the manual step of translating AIDLC artefacts into ADO work items. The linkage between requirements.md and specific ADO work items provides complete traceability.

Setup required: The ADO-Copilot integration needs to be configured by your tech lead or DevOps team. This is a one-time setup per organisation, not per repo.

Jenkins (CI/CD)
Developers write Jenkinsfiles manually or use templates. Deployments triggered by PR merges.
AIDLC Operations phase generates IaC and deployment configuration. Jenkins pipelines remain unchanged structurally; AI-generated configs are reviewed before applying.
⟳ Jenkins unchanged in structure ⚠ Review AI-generated Jenkinsfiles carefully

Jenkins remains your CI/CD engine. AIDLC doesn't replace Jenkins — it generates configuration for Jenkins to execute. The key risk: AI-generated Jenkinsfiles must be reviewed with the same rigour as application code. AI can introduce pipeline steps that bypass security scanning gates or mishandle credentials. Your DevOps team should define non-negotiable pipeline requirements in the steering rules.

MS365 / Teams
Teams calls for sprint ceremonies, ad-hoc chat, document sharing in SharePoint.
Teams calls host Mob Elaboration and Mob Construction sessions. Copilot for M365 assists with stakeholder communications. SharePoint mirrors AIDLC artefacts for non-technical stakeholders.
✓ No new collaboration tool needed Compatible with our stack

Our MS365 investment works well with AIDLC. Mob sessions run on Teams. Copilot for M365 (if licensed) can assist POs in preparing intent statements, summarising Mob session outputs for stakeholders, and drafting stakeholder communications about feature changes.

Optional enhancement: Use SharePoint to publish a read-only view of aidlc-docs/inception/requirements.md for business stakeholders who need visibility without accessing the Azure DevOps repo.

📊 Metrics & Measurement
Traditional SDLC / Agile-Scrum
AIDLC (AI-Driven)
Velocity / throughput
Story points per sprint. Team-relative, not comparable across teams. Easy to game.
Cycle time per work item (creation to production). Features per Bolt. AI-estimated complexity vs actual.
✓ More objective metrics ⚠ New measurement system needed
Quality
Defect rate, test coverage. Measured per sprint or release.
Defect rate, test coverage, plus AI accuracy rate (% of AI artefacts requiring major rework), approval latency (time AI waits for human input).
✓ More granular quality visibility AIDLC wins

AIDLC introduces new quality metrics specific to AI-human collaboration: AI accuracy rate (how often does AI get it right on the first pass?) and approval latency (how long does AI wait for human decisions?). These metrics help teams continuously improve their steering rules and collaboration patterns.

Recommended frameworks: DORA metrics (deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, time to restore) remain directly applicable. SPACE framework (satisfaction, performance, activity, collaboration, efficiency) provides a holistic view of developer experience under AIDLC.

Productivity measurement
Lines of code, story points completed, PRs merged. Easily gamed. Doesn't capture quality.
Features shipped, cycle time, defect escape rate, developer satisfaction eNPS. Outcome-focused.
✓ Harder to game ⚠ Perceived vs actual productivity gap (see notes)

The productivity paradox: Early research (AWS, early 2025) found that teams using AI tools in traditional SDLC workflows actually measured 20% lower productivity than teams without AI — despite feeling more productive. The reason: AI tools increased the volume of code generated, but reduced code quality and increased review burden, resulting in more defects and rework.

AIDLC's answer: The methodology is specifically designed to prevent this. By requiring AI to plan before implementing, ask questions before generating, and require human approval at every gate, AIDLC maintains code quality while increasing velocity. But it only works if teams actually enforce the review standards.

Measure outcomes, not activity: Never measure AI productivity by prompts sent, tokens generated, or lines of code produced. These are easy to inflate without improving outcomes.

🔐 Governance, Security & Compliance
Traditional SDLC / Agile-Scrum
AIDLC (AI-Driven)
Approval gates
PR approvals, sprint acceptance, release sign-off. Approval context is implicit (reviewer reads code).
Explicit phase-transition gates: Inception → Construction → Operations. Each requires named human approval logged in audit.md.
✓ Clearer accountability AIDLC wins for compliance
Security practices
SAST/DAST in Jenkins pipeline. Developers responsible for secure coding. Security review for sensitive changes.
SAST/DAST unchanged and mandatory. Security standards encoded in steering rules. AI applies them consistently — but AI-generated code requires the same security review as human code.
✓ Consistent standard application ⚠ AI may reproduce vulnerable patterns from training data

Critical rule: Do not reduce or bypass security scanning for AI-generated code. AI can and does generate code with security vulnerabilities — it applies patterns from its training data, which includes vulnerable code. Your existing Jenkins SAST/DAST pipeline is your safety net.

AI advantage on security: GitHub Copilot includes real-time security vulnerability detection in its code suggestions. It can flag issues as they're generated rather than only at pipeline time. Use this as a supplement, not a replacement.

Data privacy: Never include secrets, credentials, PII, or customer data in AI prompts. Treat prompts as if they're being committed to a public GitHub repo — because they're logged in audit.md, which is committed to your version-controlled repo.

Compliance & traceability
Requirement traceability manually maintained. Often gaps between requirement, code, and test. Audit evidence requires manual assembly.
Automatic traceability: requirement → design → code → test, all linked via aidlc-docs/ and audit.md. Committed to version control.
✓ Automatic traceability matrix AIDLC wins

AIDLC may significantly improve your compliance posture for frameworks like SOC 2, ISO 27001, and GDPR. The automatic audit trail and traceability matrix are typically expensive to produce manually in traditional SDLC. With AIDLC, they are a by-product of the development process itself.

Recommendation: Before your pilot, brief your information security and compliance teams on AIDLC's audit capabilities. They may have specific requirements for how audit.md content is retained and evidenced.
